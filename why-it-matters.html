Beyond the Ocean

Why Do Coral Reefs Matter?

The health of coral reefs is intrinsically linked to human wellbeing—from food security to coastal protection to the air we breathe.

Underwater Impact

The Foundation of Marine Life

Biodiversity Hotspots

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor, yet they support approximately 25% of all marine species. This extraordinary concentration of life makes reefs the most biodiverse ecosystems in the ocean—rivaling tropical rainforests on land.

Reefs serve as nurseries for countless fish species, including many that are commercially important. They protect the building blocks of marine food chains, from microscopic plankton to apex predators. When reef ecosystems collapse, the ripple effects extend throughout the ocean.

Human Impact

How Reefs Sustain Billions

Food Security

Millions of people rely on reef fisheries to feed their families. In many coastal communities, fish from reef ecosystems provide the primary source of protein. A decline in reef biodiversity puts hundreds of thousands of families at risk of food insecurity and malnutrition.

Economic Livelihood

Communities near reef systems like the Great Barrier Reef depend heavily on tourism revenue. Visitors support local businesses, creating jobs and sustaining entire regional economies. The Great Barrier Reef alone contributes over $6 billion annually to Australia's economy.

Coastal Protection

Coral reefs act as natural breakwaters, absorbing up to 97% of wave energy and protecting coastlines from storm damage and erosion. As tropical storms increase in frequency and intensity, this protection becomes increasingly vital to coastal communities.

Medical Research

Compounds from reef organisms are being developed into treatments for cancer, arthritis, bacterial infections, and heart disease. Many potential medicines remain undiscovered. Losing reef biodiversity means losing potential medical breakthroughs.

Case Study

The Great Barrier Reef & Local Communities

Communities along Queensland's coast have built their livelihoods around the Great Barrier Reef for generations. Tourism operators, fishing families, and small business owners all depend on a healthy reef ecosystem.

Tourists don't just support reef tours—they shop locally, eat at restaurants, and patronize small businesses throughout coastal communities. As sea levels rise and bleaching events intensify, these communities face an uncertain future.

Providing resources and support to help these communities adapt to climate change is crucial. Their wellbeing is directly tied to the health of the reef that has sustained them for so long.

Global Impact

The Bigger Picture

While phytoplankton produces the majority of the ocean's oxygen, healthy reef ecosystems play an important role in the global carbon cycle and marine oxygen production. More critically, reefs serve as indicators of overall ocean health.

The same forces destroying reefs—warming, acidification, pollution—are affecting marine ecosystems worldwide. What we see happening to coral is a warning sign for the entire ocean system that produces much of the oxygen we breathe and regulates our planet's climate.

Protecting coral reefs isn't just about saving beautiful underwater landscapes. It's about maintaining the ocean systems that make life on Earth possible.

Take Action

What Can You Do?

Understanding the science is the first step. But protecting coral reefs requires action at every level—from individual choices to global policy changes.

Reduce Your Carbon Footprint

The single biggest threat to reefs is climate change. Reducing energy use, choosing sustainable transportation, and supporting clean energy all help slow warming and acidification.

Make Sustainable Choices

Choose reef-safe sunscreen, sustainable seafood, and reduce single-use plastics. Small choices add up when millions of people make them.

Support Conservation

Organizations like the Coral Reef Alliance, Great Barrier Reef Foundation, and NOAA's coral programs work to protect and restore reef ecosystems worldwide.
