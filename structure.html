The Building Blocks

The Structure of Coral

Understanding the chemistry behind coral skeletons—and how ocean acidification threatens their very formation.

The Foundation

Calcium Carbonate: The Building Block

The calcium carbonate molecule (CaCO₃) is the primary component and basic "building block" of all coral skeletons. It's the same compound found in seashells, limestone, and chalk.

The molecule consists of a calcium atom bonded to a carbonate group—one carbon atom surrounded by three oxygen atoms. Coral polyps extract calcium and carbonate ions from seawater to build their intricate structures through a process called calcification.

This process is energy-intensive and highly sensitive to ocean chemistry. When the availability of carbonate ions decreases—as happens with ocean acidification—corals struggle to build and maintain their skeletons.

The Chemistry

Understanding Carbonate

Chemical Structure

Carbonate (CO₃²⁻) is a polyatomic ion with an overall charge of -2. It has a trigonal planar structure, meaning the three oxygen atoms are arranged in a flat triangle around the central carbon atom. This geometry allows for resonance stabilization of the negative charges.

Reactivity

Carbonate typically acts as a weak base, meaning it can accept protons (H⁺ ions). When it reacts with acids, it produces carbon dioxide, water, and a salt. When heated, carbonate compounds decompose into a metal oxide and carbon dioxide gas.

The pH Buffer System

Carbonate helps "buffer" ocean pH through its conjugate acid-base pair: carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) and bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻). This system normally keeps ocean pH stable, but excess CO₂ is overwhelming this natural balance.

The Process

How Corals Build Their Skeletons

Calcification: Building a Reef

Corals extract calcium ions (Ca²⁺) and carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) from the surrounding seawater to form calcium carbonate crystals. This process, called calcification, requires significant energy—supplied by the symbiotic algae living within coral tissue.

The calcium carbonate is deposited in a crystalline form called aragonite, which builds up over time to create the hard coral skeleton. A single coral polyp is tiny, but working together over centuries, coral colonies construct the massive reef structures we see today.

The Carbonate Challenge

As ocean pH drops due to acidification, the concentration of available carbonate ions decreases. This happens because excess hydrogen ions (H⁺) in the water react with carbonate ions to form bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻).

CO₃²⁻ + H⁺ → HCO₃⁻

With fewer carbonate ions available, corals must expend more energy to calcify—energy that would otherwise go toward growth, reproduction, and stress resistance. In extreme cases, the water can become corrosive enough to dissolve existing coral skeletons.

Key Concepts

The Energy Connection

Energy Governs Stability

The shift in equilibrium from carbonate to bicarbonate occurs because bicarbonate is lower in energy (more stable) than carbonate. Bicarbonate has a lower charge density, so hydrogen ions don't distort its electron cloud as easily.

Temperature Effects

Warmer waters increase stress on the equilibrium system. Heat energy favors reverse reactions, so carbonate concentration decreases further as it's even more likely to react with hydrogen ions and produce bicarbonate.

Double Threat

This creates a double threat for corals: rising temperatures cause bleaching (expelling their energy-providing algae), while acidification makes calcification harder—requiring more energy at exactly the time when corals have less of it.
