The Chemistry of Change

Carbon Dioxide & Ocean Acidification

How atmospheric CO₂ dissolves into our oceans and triggers the chemical reactions that threaten marine ecosystems.

The Greenhouse Gas

Understanding Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is essential for keeping our planet warm enough to sustain life. It's a natural part of Earth's carbon cycle, exchanged between the atmosphere, oceans, and living organisms.

The molecule consists of a central carbon atom covalently bonded to two oxygen atoms in a linear arrangement. Under standard conditions, it appears as a colorless, odorless gas—invisible but powerful in its effects on climate.

The problem isn't CO₂ itself—it's the unprecedented rate at which human activities are adding it to the atmosphere. Since the Industrial Revolution atmospheric CO₂ has risen by roughly 50% (see global records), and the oceans have absorbed about 25–30% of emitted CO₂, reducing pH and altering marine chemistry [8].

The Process

From Atmosphere to Ocean

When CO₂ enters the ocean, it triggers a cascade of chemical reactions that ultimately increase acidity and reduce the carbonate ions that corals need to survive.

1

Atmospheric Diffusion

CO₂ moves down its concentration gradient from the atmosphere into the ocean. Because oceans have a lower concentration of dissolved CO₂ than the atmosphere, gas naturally diffuses across the air-water interface. Particles are in constant motion due to kinetic energy, driving this diffusion process.

2

Dissolution & Reaction

Once dissolved, intermolecular forces form between CO₂ and water molecules. CO₂ reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)—an unstable compound that quickly dissociates.

CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃
3

Hydrogen Ion Release

Carbonic acid breaks into hydrogen ions (H⁺) and bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻). This represents a lower energy, more stable form of carbon in water. The excess hydrogen ions are what make the water more acidic.

H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
4

Carbonate Depletion

Hydrogen ions are positively charged, so they're strongly attracted to negatively charged carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻). They react to form more bicarbonate, reducing the carbonate available for coral calcification.

H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ ⇌ HCO₃⁻

— NOAA Ocean Acidification Program
The Chemistry Details

Why Equilibrium Matters

The Carbonate Buffer System

The reactions between CO₂, carbonic acid, bicarbonate, and carbonate form what's called the carbonate buffer system. Under normal conditions, this system helps regulate ocean pH, keeping it stable for marine life.

However, when excess CO₂ enters the system, it shifts the equilibrium and increases hydrogen ion concentration, leading to ocean acidification. Since pre-industrial times, surface ocean pH has decreased by about 0.1 units — which corresponds to roughly a 25–30% increase in hydrogen ion concentration [8].

Carbonic Acid (H₂CO₃)

An unstable molecule that dissociates rapidly in water. It forms when CO₂ first dissolves but quickly breaks apart into hydrogen and bicarbonate ions.

Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)

The -1 charge delocalized across oxygen atoms makes bicarbonate much more stable and soluble than carbonic acid. It forms strong hydrogen bonds with water due to its H-O bond, making it the dominant form of dissolved carbon in seawater.

Carbonate (CO₃²⁻)

The -2 charge gives carbonate resonance stability but also makes it more reactive than bicarbonate. This high reactivity is why it easily reacts with excess hydrogen ions—and also why it readily combines with calcium to form coral skeletons.

The Temperature Factor

Heat Makes It Worse

The relationship between energy and chemical equilibrium creates a compounding problem for corals. Warmer temperatures add energy to the system, favoring the reverse reactions in the carbonate buffer system.

This means that in warmer water, carbonate ions are even more likely to react with hydrogen ions and convert to bicarbonate. Carbonate concentration decreases further at exactly the time when rising temperatures are already stressing corals through bleaching.

It's a cruel double bind: warming oceans cause bleaching while simultaneously making it harder for any surviving corals to rebuild their skeletons.
