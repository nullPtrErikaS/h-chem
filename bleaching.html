When Coral Turns White

Algae, Stressors & Bleaching

The delicate symbiosis that gives coral its color—and what happens when that partnership breaks down under stress.

The Partnership

Coral and Zooxanthellae

The vibrant colors of coral reefs come not from the corals themselves, but from microscopic algae called zooxanthellae that live within coral tissue. This is one of nature's most important partnerships—a mutualistic relationship where both organisms benefit.

The coral polyps provide protection and the ingredients necessary for photosynthesis: water, carbon dioxide, and access to sunlight. In return, the zooxanthellae provide the coral with oxygen, help remove waste, and most critically, supply energy in the form of sugars and proteins.

Up to 90% of a coral's energy needs come from this symbiotic relationship. This energy powers growth, reproduction, and the calcification process that builds coral skeletons. Without their algae partners, corals can survive for a time—but they cannot thrive.

That's an alarming statistic—but what does it actually mean? Understanding bleaching is key to understanding the crisis facing our reefs.

The Threats

What Causes Bleaching?

Various stressors can inhibit algae growth or cause their total expulsion from coral tissue. When this happens, corals lose their color and turn white—hence the term "bleaching."

Ocean Warming

Rising temperatures cause heat stress that triggers algae expulsion

Ocean Acidification

Lower pH weakens corals and disrupts the symbiotic relationship

Overfishing

Removes species that help maintain reef ecosystem balance

Pollution

Nutrient runoff and chemicals poison coral tissue

The Process

What Happens During Bleaching

When corals experience stress—particularly from elevated temperatures—they expel their zooxanthellae. Without the pigmented algae, the white calcium carbonate skeleton becomes visible through the transparent coral tissue, giving bleached coral its ghostly appearance.

Importantly, bleaching doesn't immediately kill coral. Bleached corals are alive but severely weakened. They've lost their primary energy source and are now vulnerable to disease, unable to grow, and less likely to reproduce successfully.

If conditions improve quickly—within days to weeks—corals can reabsorb algae and recover. But prolonged stress leads to starvation and death.

"Mass coral bleaching is now occurring five times more frequently than it did in the 1980s."
— Global Coral Reef Monitoring Network, 2021
The Danger

A Narrowing Window

The Reversibility Problem

Bleaching events have historically come and gone in waves, particularly tied to temperature fluctuations. Corals that bleach during summer heat can often recover in cooler months when they reaccept their algae partners.

But here's the critical problem: as ocean temperatures rise consistently year after year, the recovery windows are shrinking. What used to be "cool season" temperatures are now often still too warm for full recovery.

We're approaching a threshold where even the coolest months may exceed coral tolerance limits. When corals can no longer recover between bleaching events, reef systems begin a spiral of decline that becomes increasingly difficult to reverse.

Energy Crisis

Without algae, corals lose up to 90% of their energy supply. They can survive on stored reserves and limited feeding for weeks, but not indefinitely.

Compounding Stress

Bleached corals are weakened and more susceptible to disease. Ocean acidification simultaneously makes rebuilding skeletons harder, even if algae return.

Cascade Effects

When corals die, the entire reef ecosystem suffers. Fish lose habitat, predator-prey relationships collapse, and biodiversity plummets.
