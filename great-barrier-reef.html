The Rainforest of the Ocean

The Great Barrier Reef

The largest coral reef system on Earth, stretching over 2,300 kilometers off the coast of Australia—and one of the ecosystems most threatened by climate change.

The Australian Government's Reef 2050 Plan is a long-term framework for managing and protecting the Great Barrier Reef; it combines reef monitoring, water quality programs, and partnership with Traditional Owners to improve reef resilience [7].

Individual Reefs

UNESCO World Heritage Site

Due to their immense biodiversity, coral reefs are often referred to as "the rainforests of the ocean." The Great Barrier Reef in particular is the largest coral reef system on Earth, located off the coast of Queensland, Australia.

It's home to over 400 types of coral, 1,500 species of fish, and 4,000 types of mollusc. Within the system there are over 2,500 individual reefs, and 900 islands and cays. This extraordinary biodiversity earned it designation as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1981 [2].

An Ecosystem Under Pressure

This extraordinary ecosystem supports an intricate web of marine life, from microscopic organisms to large predators like sharks and sea turtles. All of these species are dependent on the health of the coral that forms the reef's foundation.

But over the past three decades, the Great Barrier Reef has experienced increasingly severe bleaching events that threaten its very existence. Rising ocean temperatures and acidification are pushing this ecosystem to its limits.

A History of Stress

Major Bleaching Events

The Great Barrier Reef has experienced increasingly severe bleaching events over the past three decades. Regional surveys and monitoring programs document event severity and impacts — see AIMS for event reports and summaries [1].

1998
First Mass Bleaching

Global Coral Crisis Begins

The first recorded mass bleaching event on the Great Barrier Reef, coinciding with a major El Niño event that raised ocean temperatures worldwide.

2002
Severe Bleaching

Widespread Damage

74% of inshore and 42% of offshore reefs experienced bleaching during this significant event, marking the second mass bleaching in just four years.

2006
Highly Localized

Keppel Islands Impact

98% of the reef around the inshore Keppel Islands experienced bleaching, with a 39% mortality rate among affected corals.

2009
Freshwater Bleaching

Rainfall-Induced Stress

Heavy rainfall associated with climate change flooded freshwater to nearshore reefs, causing localized bleaching from salinity stress.

2011
Freshwater Bleaching

Repeated Rainfall Impact

Similar to 2009, more heavy rainfall occurred this year, resulting in localized bleaching as climate patterns continued to intensify.

2016
Catastrophic Event

Worst Bleaching on Record

93% of the reef affected. The northern third lost nearly 30% of its shallow-water corals—the most severe event ever documented on the Great Barrier Reef.

2017
Back-to-Back Crisis

Consecutive Mass Bleaching

For the first time in recorded history, the reef experienced mass bleaching in consecutive years, preventing recovery from the devastating 2016 event.

2020
Third Mass Event

Most Widespread Yet

Bleaching reached the southern reef for the first time, affecting all three regions of the Great Barrier Reef simultaneously.

2022
La Niña Bleaching

Unprecedented Timing

Mass bleaching occurred during a La Niña year—typically a cooler period—indicating that even "cool" conditions now exceed coral tolerance thresholds.

2024
Fifth in Nine Years

Ongoing Crisis

The reef's fifth mass bleaching event in just nine years, with the warmest February on record contributing to widespread damage across the entire system.
